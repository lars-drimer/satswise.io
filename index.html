The 10 Tips

10 habits of high-signal stackers

Are you being satswise?

Most Bitcoiners are quietly bleeding sats — through exchange fees they don't notice, custody setups that introduce risk, and timing habits that cost them more than they realize. This isn't a beginner's guide to Bitcoin. It's a sharper playbook for people who already get it and want to accumulate more efficiently.

Published May 31, 2026 · 10 tips · 8 min read

01

Stop paying retail exchange fees

Most retail exchanges make the bulk of their margin on spread — the gap between the buy and sell price — not just the listed fee. You might see "0.5% fee" and feel fine, then quietly get another 0.3–0.8% clipped on the spread before your order even fills. Over a year of regular buying, that's a meaningful chunk of sats walking out the door.

The fix is simple: use a professional-grade exchange like Kraken that offers a real order book with maker/taker fees. Place limit orders instead of market orders — you become the maker, not the taker, and fees drop to near zero. A 0.16% maker fee versus a 1.5% all-in retail rate is not a rounding error; it's real sats compounding over years.

If you're doing DCA-style buys, batch your purchases to reduce the number of transactions. Frequency is fine emotionally, but weekly beats daily for fee efficiency if the amounts are small.

Action step: Log into whatever exchange you use now and look up your actual all-in cost including spread. If you can't find that number easily, that's the answer.
02

Automate your DCA — but do it with a plan

Dollar-cost averaging works, but "just buy every week" is not a plan — it's a default. The first thing to nail down is your DCA base: what percentage of your income are you committing? Most people who think seriously about this land somewhere between 5–20% of take-home, depending on their situation. Decide on the number, automate it, and stop revisiting it every time the price moves.

Apps like Swan Bitcoin and similar dedicated stacking platforms are built specifically for this workflow. They tend to have lower fees than generalist exchanges on recurring buys, they auto-withdraw to your wallet, and they don't try to upsell you on altcoins. That focus matters.

One underrated move: align your DCA date to when your paycheck hits. There's evidence that buying on a fixed schedule regardless of price beats trying to time dips — not because timing is impossible, but because the mental overhead of timing usually leads to buying less overall.

Action step: Set up a recurring buy for a fixed amount this week, timed to hit 1–2 days after your income arrives. Set it and ignore it.
03

Get your sats off exchanges — properly

You know this. And yet, more Bitcoin sits on exchanges than should. The risk isn't hypothetical — exchange insolvency, withdrawal freezes, and hacks are recurring events in this industry. Not your keys, not your coins is a cliché because it keeps being proven right.

If you haven't moved to self-custody yet, the barrier is lower than it used to be. A hardware wallet like Trezor is the minimum bar for any meaningful holding. The setup takes an afternoon. The peace of mind is permanent. The real cost of not doing it is the tail risk of losing everything to a counterparty you can't control.

One thing people overlook: consolidate your UTXOs before withdrawing from an exchange. Withdrawing in many small batches is inefficient when on-chain fees spike. Pull in larger, less frequent withdrawals and your on-chain fee burden drops substantially.

Action step: If you don't have a hardware wallet yet, order one this week. If you do, check when you last verified your seed phrase backup is readable and in a safe location.
04

Learn the mempool before you send

On-chain Bitcoin fees are not fixed — they're a market. During high-activity periods, transaction fees can spike 10–50x compared to quiet periods. Sending on a Saturday evening during a bull run can cost you the same as sending during a slow Sunday morning a year later. If you're not looking at the mempool before you send, you're accepting whatever the wallet defaults suggest, which are often optimistic.

mempool.space is the tool here. It shows you the current fee environment, what's in the mempool, and — crucially — the projected wait time at different fee rates. If your transaction isn't time-sensitive, you can set a low fee and wait it out. Blocks come every ~10 minutes; if you can wait a few hours, you can often pay 5–10x less.

If your wallet supports it, use RBF (Replace-By-Fee) on every non-urgent transaction. It gives you the option to bump the fee later if it stalls, without re-sending from scratch. Most modern wallets default to this — if yours doesn't, that's worth knowing.

Action step: Before your next on-chain send, open mempool.space and check the current fee rate. Compare it to what your wallet is suggesting. You may be surprised.
05

Use Lightning for small amounts — stop paying on-chain fees for coffee money

If you're moving small amounts of Bitcoin — tipping, paying for services, settling micro-invoices — doing it on-chain is economically irrational once fees are even modestly elevated. A 5,000 sat transaction with a 1,000 sat fee is a 20% haircut. That's not stacking; that's bleeding.

Lightning solves this for small, frequent transactions. Fees on Lightning are measured in millisatoshis. You can move 1,000 sats for fractions of a sat in fees. The UX has improved dramatically — wallets like Phoenix, Wallet of Satoshi, or Breez handle the channel management for you, so you're not manually managing liquidity unless you want to.

A hybrid setup works well: keep your long-term stack in cold storage, keep a smaller Lightning wallet topped up for day-to-day transactions. Think of it like a checking account versus a brokerage account. Don't route everything through Lightning, but don't pay on-chain fees for small amounts either.

Action step: Install a Lightning wallet (Phoenix or Wallet of Satoshi are the easiest starts) and send yourself a small amount via Lightning. Get comfortable with the flow before you need it.
06

Buy peer-to-peer when it makes sense

P2P platforms like Bisq, RoboSats, and Peach Bitcoin let you buy directly from other individuals — often at prices close to or at spot, without KYC requirements and without giving a centralized entity custody of your transaction data. The privacy benefit is real, and for some buyers the pricing is better than retail exchanges once you factor in fees.

P2P isn't for every purchase. The UX is more involved, the liquidity is thinner, and you need to know what you're doing with escrow flows. But for periodic larger buys — or if you're in a jurisdiction with poor exchange access — it's worth understanding. The skill of buying P2P is an asset on its own.

One practical tip: P2P platforms often have a reputation system. Sellers with high trade counts and long histories will command slightly higher prices, but they're worth it. Don't chase the cheapest offer from an account with two trades and a new registration date.

Action step: Browse Bisq or RoboSats just to understand how they work. You don't have to buy anything — knowing your options is part of being satswise.
07

Track your cost basis from day one

Tax treatment of Bitcoin varies by jurisdiction, but in most places it's treated as property — meaning every sale, trade, or spend is a taxable event. If you're not tracking your cost basis meticulously, you're either overpaying taxes (claiming a higher gain than you actually made) or setting yourself up for an audit. Either way, it's a leak.

The fix is boring but important: track every transaction. Tools like Koinly can import from most exchanges and wallets automatically, reconcile across platforms, and spit out a tax report. The cost of the software is trivially small compared to the cost of getting your taxes wrong.

If you're in the US, understand the difference between short-term and long-term capital gains before you sell anything. Holding for over a year often means paying roughly half the tax rate on gains. That's not a loophole — it's the law working as designed, and ignoring it is leaving sats on the table.

Action step: Connect at least one exchange to a tax tracking tool this week, even if tax season is months away. Retroactive reconciliation is much harder than staying current.
08

Don't let UTXO management become a fee sink

Bitcoin is UTXO-based, which means your wallet's "balance" is actually a collection of discrete unspent outputs — each one an independent coin. If you've been buying small amounts over time without consolidating, you might have hundreds of tiny UTXOs. When you go to spend or move funds and fees are high, you pay for each input. That wallet with 300 UTXOs will cost you significantly more to empty than one with 10.

UTXO consolidation — combining many small outputs into fewer larger ones — is best done during low-fee periods. When the mempool is quiet and fees are at 2–5 sat/vbyte, that's the time to sweep and consolidate. You pay a small fee now to avoid a large aggregated fee later when it matters more.

This is also why choosing a wallet with good UTXO management visibility matters. Sparrow Wallet is excellent for this — it shows you exactly what your UTXOs look like, lets you manually select inputs, and gives you the control to manage consolidation deliberately.

Action step: Open your Bitcoin wallet and look at how many UTXOs you have. If it's more than 20–30 and they're small, flag "consolidate during next low-fee period" in your calendar.
09

Recognize scams before they recognize you

This might feel basic, but Bitcoin scams are increasingly sophisticated and the amounts lost annually are staggering — billions of dollars across pig butchering schemes, fake exchanges, and recovery scams. Smart people lose Bitcoin to these. It doesn't happen because people are stupid; it happens because the attackers are patient and know exactly which emotional levers to pull.

A few hard rules that don't have exceptions: no one offering you guaranteed Bitcoin returns is legitimate. No exchange or wallet will ask for your seed phrase. Any "recovery service" for lost Bitcoin is a second scam targeting people already burned by the first one. If someone contacts you about an investment opportunity, assume fraud until proven otherwise.

The subtler version of this is more important: protect your stack from yourself. Signing transactions in haste, entering seed phrases into software you didn't vet, or connecting hardware wallets to sketchy dApps are all ways competent Bitcoiners lose funds. Slow down every time you interact with your keys. The irreversibility of Bitcoin is its best feature and its most punishing flaw simultaneously.

Action step: Tell one person in your life who owns Bitcoin about the pig butchering / romance scam pattern specifically. Awareness is the best defense.
10

Compound your smallest amounts — satoshis add up

There's a psychological failure mode in Bitcoin stacking where people ignore small amounts because they feel trivial. A few thousand sats here, a Lightning cashback reward there — it doesn't feel like it matters. But satoshis are not infinite. There will only ever be 2.1 quadrillion of them, and with global adoption, even a single sat will eventually be worth something meaningful. Don't throw them away.

Practical version: if you earn sats through cashback apps, Lightning gaming, or micro-payments, sweep them regularly into your cold storage rather than spending them reflexively. Set a threshold — say, once your Lightning wallet hits 100,000 sats — and move them to cold. The frequency trains the habit; the habit compounds over years.

The broader mindset shift is treating sats like equity, not cash. Would you spend your Berkshire Hathaway shares on lunch? Your sats have the same scarcity property. Spending them is a choice you should make deliberately, not out of convenience. Earn in fiat, stack in sats, spend in fiat where you can. That's the loop.

Action step: Check every account you have for any unredeemed sats — cashback apps, gaming wallets, faucets, old test wallets. Sweep them all into cold storage this week. Every sat counts.

Stack smarter, not just harder

Most of these habits cost nothing to implement — they just require attention. The Bitcoiners who accumulate the most over time aren't necessarily the ones who bought earliest or have the most capital. They're the ones who minimize leakage, automate the boring parts, and stay patient while everyone else gets distracted.

That's what being satswise means.
